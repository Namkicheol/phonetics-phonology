Phonology


1. Distribution


1) Allophone vs phoneme


- Phoneme: abstract, underlying unit of a sound (mental representation)


- Allophone: perceivable sounds corresponding to the phoneme in various environments.


2) Overlapping distribution: when two sounds are capable of occurring in the same environment


- non-predictable distribution: one environment will be enough to conclude the overlap.


- Contrast(ive distribution): when two sounds are found in an overlapping distribution and the substitution of one sound for
the other changes the meaning of the word.  manifestations of different phonemes


- Minimal pairs: pairs of words that have exactly the same sound sin the same order except for a single difference in sounds
and have different meanings. (overlapping + contrast)


-near-minimal pair: a pair of words that would be a minimal pair except for some irrelevant difference


3) Complementary distribution: never find the two or more sounds in the same environment (/X/ never appears in any of the
phonetic environments in which /Y/ occurs, mutually exclusive


- Allophone: two or more sounds are allophone (positional variants) of the same phoneme if (a) they are in complementary
distribution and (b) they are phonetically similar.


4) Natural classes: sets of sounds that share the same value for a feature set or sets of features.


5) Free variation


① Allophonic free variation: the allophones of a same phoneme occur in the same environment. [bek] vs [bek [̚ ] ]


② Phonemic free variation: the sounds belonging to separate phonemes(allophones of different phonemes), overlapping


distribution without change in meaning ex. economics, either, neither

1) Stops                                *fortis: voiceless, lenis: voiced


- devoicing


-voiced stops /b,d,g/: intervocalic  fully voiced, initial&final  partially devoiced


-final devoicing is greater than initial devoicing. final devoicing is present only if there is no voiced sound coming
immediately after, if there is a voiced sound immediately a after, devoicing does not take place.


- weak aspiration: (a) in an unstressed syllable(polite), (b) before a syllabic consonant(pickle), (c) if released in final position


- no audible release: syllable-final single coda stops


-when we have two non-homorganic stops in a row, there is no audible release for the first stop. The closure of the second
stop in sequence is made before the release of the first stop. Ex. sipped


-When we have two homorganic, there is no separate release for the first stop, rather, there is one prolonged closure for the
two stops in question. Ex. big girl, black kat, top block, white dog


- Nasal plosion: the stop closure is maintained and nasally released in cases in which the stops is followed by a homorganic
nasal. The air pressure is released through the nasal cavity. (a) syllabic nasal(button), (b) nasal is in initial position of the
following syllable of the word(submarine), (c) the nasal in the initial position of the next word (sad news)


- Lateral plosion: the stop closure is maintained and laterally released when the stop is followed by a homorganic lateral. Ex.
cattle, at last





































3. English Consonants




2) Fricatives


- voiced fricatives /v, z, ð, ʒ/: intervocalic  fully voiced, initial&final  partially devoiced


- when a word ends in a fricative and the next word starts with the same fricative, we get one longer narrow vocal tract. Ex.
tennis socks, half full


- Sibilants: /s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ/


-/ʃ, ʒ/ have an appreciable lip rounding (labialization)


-/ʒ/ is well established in medial and final position but not found in word-initial.


- Glottal /h/ can only appear in syllable-initial position-(a) voiceless in word-initial (b) breathy voice in intervocalic


- /ð/ is restricted to grammatical morpheme. Unstressed initial /ð/ becomes assimilated to previous alveolar consonant.


3) Affricates


- /dʒ/ in intervocalic: fully voiced, in initial and final position: partially voiced


- Affricates have separate release in cases of two adjacent identical segments unlike stops and fricatives. Ex. much cheaper,
orange juice, large chair


- Affricates behave like one segment and not like consonant clusters, though they are phonetically made up of two sounds.


(a) English does not allow any onsets with a stop + fricative combination


(b) Spoonerism: affricates fill the space that is located by a single segment when transported from one position to another.


(c) Sounds representing an affricate are noticeably shorter than the sequence of the sounds that make up affricates.


*Lenis obstruents are voiced only when they occur intervocalically. They are partially voiced in initial and final position,
unless followed by a voiced sound.


*Vowel lengthening
a. Vowels are longer before voiced consonants than before their voiceless counterparts
b. vowels are longer before sonorant consonants than before obstruent
c. vowels are longer in open syllable than in closed syllables
+Other things being equal, vowels are longer in stressed syllables
+Other things being equal, vowels are longest in monosyllabic words (syllable has only one vowel), newt longest in words
with two syllables, and shortest in words with more than two syllables because English is stress timed language and
stressed syllable should occur at regular intervals


4) Nasals


- A nasal have one prolonged closure in the cases where it is followed by an identical nasal. Ex. ten names


- Bilabial and alveolar nasals become labio-dentals(/ɱ/) when they are followed by a labio-dental sound.


- Nasals can be syllabic in English only when it is immediately preceded by an obstruent. When the consonant preceding the
nasal is preceded by another consonant, the nasal tends not to be syllabic, as we normally insert a schwa in that syllable.


- Velar nasal restriction: velar nasal can occur only in coda position and it can only be preceded by lax vowels.


5) Approximants (liquids + glides)


- The labio-velar /w/ has two places of articulation: lip rounding (labial), the back part of the tongue is raised toward the
velum(velar)


- liquids can be syllabic in English, accepting any consonant. When the consonant preceding the liquid is preceded by another
consonant, the liquid tends not to be syllabic, as we normally insert a schwa in that syllable. Lack of requirement of
homorganicity between the syllabic liquids and the preceding consonant.

1) Assimilation

6. Syllables


1) The hierarchical internal structure of syllable


- Dependencies between nuclei and codas are commonly found: the coda that follows /au/ has to be alveolar or interdental.
This nucleus cannot be followed by labial or velar consonants.


- use of skeletal tier: reveals the timing slots for each unit. (a) distinguish branching from non-branching. (b) help us to deal
with segments such as affricates that are phonetically complex but phonologically simple.


2) Sonority: the number of syllables in a word will be equal to the number of sonority peaks in that word.


- The dimensions of sonority: (a) the degree opening of vocal tract during articulation (b) the sounds’ propensity of voicing


[Hierarchy of Sonority]
Vowels (low>mid>high, glide) > flaps > laterals > nasals > fricatives (voiced>voiceless) > stops (voiced>voiceless)
-sonority peaks: (a) vowels/diphthongs (b) sonorant consonant


3) Syllabification: where the syllable boundaries lie.


- Maximal onset principle: assign any series of intervocalic C to the syllable on the right as long as it does not violate languagespecific onset pattern. If possible, every syllable should be open.


- Syllable weight is determined by its rhyme structure.


-a syllable is light if it has a non-branching rhyme (a short vowel and no coda in its rhyme).


-it is heavy if it has a branching rhyme (a short vowel followed by a coda (simple or complex), or a long vowel or a diphthong
with or without a following coda)


-heavy syllables attract stress and no stressed syllable may be light.




- Ambisyllabicity: while the maximal onset principle dictates that first syllables of each of these words be light, the stress that
falls on this very syllable contradicts the principle that light syllables cannot receive stress. A consonant that is a permissible
onset (cluster) is ambisyllabic if it occurs immediately after a short vowel that forms the nucleus of a stressed syllable. This
is consequence of tendency for a stressed rhyme to be heavy.

7. Stress & Intonation


1) Stress: the prosodic features of duration, intensity and pitch  prominence of stressed syllables is generally manifested
by their characteristics of being longer, louder, and higher in pitch than unstressed syllables. +antepenult-penult-ult


- Syllable weight


-light: if the rhyme is non-branching (a short V and no coda)


-heavy: if the rhyme is branching (has short V, except schwa, followed by a coda, or has a long V or diphthong with or without a following coda)


2) Stress of nouns and adjectives


- In disyllabics of nouns and adjectives, the default stress is on penult. Ex. agent, balance, empire / absent, ample, perfect


-exception: (a) due to weightless penult [�], the final syllable is stressed (appeal, balloon / banal, divine), (b) They are stressed
on the final syllable despite the fact that they have stressable penults with branching rhymes (sardine, typhoon, antique /
mundane, obscure, robust, obscene)


- In trisyllabic and longer nouns: stress penult if stressable (tomato, aroma, bonanza, enigma). If not stressable, stress the next
left heavy syllable (abdomen, hippopotamus, buffalo)


-exception: (a) mostly borrowing from French, retain the original final stresses (clarinet, cavalier, engineer), (b) antepenult
stress despite the fact that they have stressable penults (alien, arduous, period), (c) words coming from place names are
stressed on the penult despite the fact that it is not heavy (Yokohama, Vienna, Kentucky, Seattle), (d) Some other place-names:
carry the stress on their non-heavy penult. They do not have any other heavy syllable to the left of penult (Morocco, Havana)


3) Stress of verbs: focus on ult. If heavy, stress ult; if not, go to the next left heavy syllable. Ex. achieve, digest/ balance,
differ


-exception: penult is stressed despite the fact that the verb has a heavy ult. Ex. copy, diagram, rescue


-In trisyllabic verbs, heavy ults are stressed, penult-stressed because of unstressable, antepenult-stressed because of
unstressable ult and unstressable penult.


4) English has orthographically identical word-pairs differentiated by stress as nouns or verbs. Ex. address, project, conduct,
rebel


- Noun-verb shift (a) by a shift in stressed syllable (import, insult), (b) by vowel reduction in unstressed syllable, and thus
noun-verb pairs, although homographs, are not homophonous. Ex. abstract, convict, protest, upset


- some have the stress on the penult and others have stress on the ult. Ex. penult: silence, triumph, harvest / ult: surprise, delay


*the difference from its specific contextual meaning. ex. defense-penult: concepts specific to sports, ult: related to the legal
and military field.


5) Secondary stress: there is prominence of more than one syllable. Ex. photographic


- In general, secondary stress comes before the primary stress (major pitch change tends
to be on the last stressed syllable)


6) Adding suffixes lead to various effects on word stress


(a) stress-bearing (attracting) suffix: attract stress to themselves, always constitute
heavy syllables. Ex. lemonade, millionaire, realization, absentee, mountaineer,
Japanese, picturesque, kitchenette, laryngitis, honorific


(b) stress-neutral suffixes: never make any difference to the stress pattern of the resulting word. Include all eight inflectional
suffixes. Ex. arrival, ascendant, celibacy, freedom, player, lioness, nationhood, graceful, greenish, alcoholism, humanist,
submissive, specialize, friendly, bottomless, friendly, amendment, frankness, friendship, burdensome, clockwise, growth,
certainty, silky


*Adjective-forming suffix -y should not be treated in the same way as noun-forming -y, which shifts the stress to the antepenultimate. Ex. homophone – homophony, photograph-photography


*When several unstressed syllables are piled up to the right of the stress, the stress moves to antepenult. Ex. moment 
momentary  momentarily


(c) Stress-shifting (fixing) suffixes: when added to a root, shift the stress from its original position to the syllable immediately
preceding the suffix. Ex. Aristotelian, substantial, librarian, geometrical, insecticide, periodic, personify, laborious, humidity,
speedometer, contextual, momentous, homonymy.


*If the original stress is on the last syllable of the root, no change in location of the stress will result because it is already it
should be. Ex. diverse  diversify, obese  obesity

8. Foot structure


1) Metrical foot: consists of a stressed syllable followed by zero or more unstressed syllables. ex. ‘ Chicken Mac’Nugget


- Foot: a prosodic unit above syllable, which consists of one obligatory strong syllable and optional weak syllables.


- trochaic: the basic rhythmic pattern consists of a stressed syllable followed by zero or more unstressed syllables. This is an
adjective derived from the noun trochee. A trochee is essentially a stressed-unstressed sequence



7) Compound: composed of more than one root morpheme, but functions like a single word in syntactic and semantic terms.


- A compound can be written (a) as single word: primary stress on first (blueprint), (b) with a hyphen in between: primary
stress on first (fail-safe), (c) with a space between elements: stress on the second element


- Noun/Adjectives compounds: stress on their first element


- Verb compounds: stress on the first element except particle/adverb + verb (undertake, oversleep, ill-treat)


- compounds with more than two-elements, complex compounds  first element


8) Intonation: can be described in terms of tone groups or intonational phrases.


- Tone group: the part of a sentence over which an intonation contour extends.


- Tonic syllable: within a tone group, each stressed syllable has a minor pitch increase, but there is one syllable in which this
pitch increase is more significant / unit of information.


- Pattern: most common pattern is to put tonic accent on the last stressed lexical item.

Morphology


1. Content words and Function words


1) Content words: denote concepts such as objects actions, attributes, and ideas. Nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs are
the content words, open class


2) Function words: specify grammatical relations and have little or no semantic content. conjunctions(and, or, but),
articles(the, a/an), pronouns(it), prepositions(in, of), closed class


2. Morphemes


1) Morpheme: the minimal linguistic unit


- Free morphemes: Some morphemes may constitute words by themselves


- Bound morphemes: Other morphemes are never words by themselves but are always parts of words. Ex. prefixes, suffixes,
infixes, circumfixes


2) Root and Stems


- A _root_ may or may not stand alone as a word (paint and read do; ceive and ling don’t’)


- When a _root_ morpheme is combined with an affix, it forms a _stem_ . Other affixes can be added to a stem to form a more
complex stem.


- Bound Roots do not occur in isolation and they acquire meaning only in combination with other morphemes


3. Word Formation


1) Derivational Morphology: When derivational affixes are added to a base, a new word with a new meaning is derived.
Derived word may also be of a different grammatical class than the original word. Some derivational affixes do not cause a
change in grammatical class.      *verb forming -en: only attached to monosyllabic adjective


2) Inflectional Morphology: Many languages, including English, also have bound morphemes that have a strictly grammatical
function. They mark properties such as tense, number, person, and so forth


-Inflectional morphemes follow the derivational morphemes in a word.


-Inflectional morphemes are productive.

4) Word formation rules


① Back-Formation : The word formation process in which an actual or supposed derivational affix detaches from the base


form of the word to create a new word. Ex. hawk, stoke, swindle, burgle, resurrect, preempt, televise


② Compounds: two or more words may be joined to form new, compound words ex. outpatient, undergo, waterbed


-Compound word vs non-compound expression: (a) compound word ( 첫 단어에 stress) (b) non-compound word ( 뒤에


stress) ex. greenhouse ‘a glass-enclosed garden’ vs green house ‘a house painted green’


③ Blending: creates words from non-morphemic parts of two already existing items, usually the first part of one and the final


part of the other. Ex. wi-fi(from wireless and hi-fi), bromance(from brother and romance), brunch(from breakfast and lunch)


④ Cliticization: Some morphemes behave like words in terms of their meaning and function but are unable to stand alone as


independent forms for phonological reasons. Ex. I’m leaving now.


⑤ Conversion: a process that assigns an already existing word to a new syntactic category ex. must, poor, empty, report


⑥ Clipping: a process that shortens a polysyllabic word by deleting one or more syllables. Ex. zoo < zoological garden


⑦ Coinage: types of brand products are used as the generic name. Ex. Xerox, Kleenex


⑧ Acronyms: formed by taking the initial letters of (some or all) the words in a phrase or title and pronouncing them as a


word ex. UNICEF: United Nations International Children’s Emergency Fund


⑨ Initialism: use initial letters and pronounced as a series of letters ex. NYU(New Yor University)

3. Classifying situations


1) Verbs


- Stative verb: These verbs allow the speaker to view a situation as a steady state, with no internal phrases or changes.
Moreover, the speaker does not overtly focus on the beginning or end of the state. Not imperatives!


- Dynamic verb: Regarding a dynamic verb like _learn_, the speaker is describing a process and focusing on the end point; at
the beginning Mary didn’t know how to drive sports cars, and at the end she has learnt


a. durative/punctual: Durative is applied to verbs which describe a situation or process which lasts for a period time in (a),
while punctual describes an event that seems instantaneous in (b)


(29) a. John slept. b. John coughed.


-A durative adverbial can trigger an iterative interpretation:


(30) a. Fred coughed _all night_ .  b. The drunk knocked _for ten minutes._ c. The cursor flashed _until the battery ran down_ .


b. telic/atelic: Telic refers to those processes which are seen as having a natural completion (Sometimes called resultatives),
while atelic doesn’t.


(31) a. Harry was building a raft.  telic     b. Harry was gazing at the sea.  atelic


(32) a. Fred was running. (atelic)    b. Fred was running in the London Marathon. (telic)


- Semantic restriction on ‘Do-so’


-The verb must be dynamic. Ex. *Robin dislikes Mary, but Leslie doesn’t do so.


-The subject must have the agent role. Ex. *He fell out of the window accidently, but she didn’t do so unexpectedly.


4. Scope Ambiguity


1) Quantifier interaction 이나 Negative expression – Quantifier interaction 이 나온다 .


- 두 가지 의미를 paraphrase 할 때, some/none/any 를 잘 활용할 것 . Paraphrase 해도 ambiguity 가 사라지지 않을 수 있기 때문 .


-Scope 이해하기 : 왼쪽에 있는 요소가 Scope 가 일반적으로 더 넓다 .


-Every … not: 전체가 모두 아니다 = None


-Not every …: 모든 게 그런 것은 아니다 = Some


(1) Every boy likes a girl.


(i) Every boy has a different girl that they like. (Every > a) (ii) There is a girl that every boy likes. (a > Every)


(2) Every student has not done their assignment.


(i) No one has done their assignment. (Every > not)    (ii) Someone has done their assignment. (Not > Every)


(3) Mary refused to visit every city that Tom visited.


(i) Mary would visit none of the city that Tom visited. (Every > refuse)


(ii) Mary would visit some cities that Tom visited. (refuse > Every)




- If vs Unless


-If…not 을 unless 로 바꾸면, ungrammatical 하거나 meaning change 가 나타난다 . 이때 meaning change 는 except in


the case that (~ 라면 모를까 ).


i. If it hadn’t been for the quick action of Jack Armstrong, we would have been involved in a terrible accident.


ii. *Unless it had been for the quick action of Jack Armstrong, we would have been involved in a terrible accident.


i. Look, I couldn’t possibly have pulled off the job and gotten all the way down here in ten minutes unless, maybe, I’d had a

helicopter. ( 헬기라도 있으면 모를까 여기에 올 수가 없다 .)


ii.≠ Look, I couldn’t possibly have pulled off the job and gotten all the way down here in ten minutes if I hadn’t had a

helicopter. ( 헬기가 있었기 때문에 여기까지 올 수 있었다 .)


Pragmatics
























- Scalar implicatures: when any form in a scale is asserted, the negative of all forms higher on the scale is implicated.


-All > most > much/many > some …. 의 scale 이 있는데 여기서 some 을 선택했다는 것은 not all, not most, not


much/many 를 imply. Quantifier 만 있는 것이 아니라 온도를 나타내는 데에도 쓰인다 . Ex. warm = not hot


2) Particularized conversational implicature: An implicature whose association with the sentence S crucially depends upon
rather specific features of the immediate context. Thus, in most imaginable contexts, sentence S does not carry the implicature
in question.


A: Are you going out tonight? B: I have to work.
 implicature: I am not going out tonight.
 key observation: in most other imaginable contexts, the sentence “I have to work” does not carry the same implicature.
A: Why are you applying for a work permit? B: I have to work.

- perlocutionary act: the effect of an illocutionary act, the effect on the audience. Ex. persuaded, impressed, sacred,
enlightened…

1) Generalized conversational implicature (scalar implicature): : Context 와 상관없이 일반적으로 함축되어지는 의미,


An implicature whose association with the sentence S depends only upon very general features of the context and/or worldknowledge. Thus, in most imaginable contexts, sentence S does carry the implicature in questions.


A: What happened to Dave after college?
B: Dage got a good job and get married (implicature= Dave got a good job first, and then later got married)
-Key observation: In most imaginable contexts, the sentence “Dave got a good job and got married carries the implicature.
A: Tell me something, anything about Dave.
B: Dave got a good job and got married.  same implicature.



2) deitic(deixis): words or expressions whose reference relies on context and the orientation of the speaker in space and time.
Require knowledge of the situation of the utterance to be interpreted referentially. ex. Pronoun, demonstratives, adverbs,
propositions, complex expressions(those towers over there)
