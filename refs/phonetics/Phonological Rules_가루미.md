**Phonological Rules**


1. Allophonic rules


1) Assimilation


①Aspiration


- 개념 : a voiceless noncontinuant has [+aspirated] added to its feature matrix at the beginning of a syllable when followed by


a stressed vowel with an optional intervening consonant.


Ex. pit, repeat[rɪpʰit]/crib, clip, quip/chip, pill [pʰɪl], apart [əpʰart], creep [kʰrip]


-Phonological rules that add nondistinctive features, which are predictable from the context. The rule that aspirates voiceless
stops at the beginning of words and syllables in English is such a rule.


②Vowel nasalization


- 개념 : vowels are nasalized before a nasal consonant within the same syllable.


a. class of sounds: vowels


b. phonetic change: change phonemic oral vowels to phonetic nasal vowels


c. context (phonological environment): before a nasal consonant within the same syllable


ex. band [bæ᷈nd] farm [fɑ᷈rm] can [kæ᷈n], boom [bũm], ban [bæ̃n], bean [bĩn]


201135
ex. gone to seed --> god to seen
After a metathesis error occurs, the first vowel in the actual utterance lost nasalization because it no longer occurs before
a nasal consonant. Instead, the third vowel in the actual utterance is nasalized because it is followed by a nasal consonant.


③Glottalization and Preglottalization


- A glottal stop: the sound that occurs when the vocal cords are held tightly together.


Ex. Batman [bæʔmæn] / he hit me [hihɪʔmi]


-the glottal stop replacement requires the target /t/ to be in a syllable-final position.


-it is also observable before the syllabic nasal. [biʔn̩ ]


- Preglottalization (glottal reinforcement): add a glottal stop before /t/   Ex. hit [hɪ ʔ t], tap [tæʔp]


④Vowel Lengthening


a. Vowels are longer before voiced consonants than before their voiceless counterparts


ex. bag > back, nab > nap


b. vowels are longer before sonorant consonants than before obstruent


ex. goal > goad


c. vowels are longer in open syllable than in closed syllables


ex. sea > seed > seat, bay > bait


*vowel length scale
in an open syllable > in a syllable closed by a sonorant consonant > in a syllable closed by a voiced consonant > in a
syllable closed by a voiceless consonant
*Other things being equal, vowels are longer in stressed syllables
Ex. belów [oʋ] > bíllow[oʋ]
*Other things being equal, vowels are longest in monosyllabic words (syllable has only one vowel), newt longest in words
with two syllables, and shortest in words with more than two syllables because English is stress timed language and
stressed
syllable should occur at regular intervals.
Ex. speed > speedy > speedily


⑤/l/-velarization


- 개념 : The liquid /l/ is velarized when it appears in rhyme position


a. clear /l/: /l/ precedes the vowel, in prevocalic (onset) position / ex. clear, like, law


b. dark /l/: /l/ follows the vowel, in postvocalic (coda) position / ex. dull, full, belt


→ in complementary distribution: clear [l] appears in onset position, and dark [ɫ] in the coda


∴ l-velarization takes place in syllable rhymes


Ex. life [lɑɪf] vs file [fɑɪɫ], feeling [filɪŋ] vs feel [fiɫ], fail [feɪɫ], milk /mɪlk/ → [mɪɫk], little /lɪtl/ → [lɪtɫ] (↔holy [holi]),


- AEP) Dialectal variation


a. In American English, the syllabic l is invariably dark. It is hard to find the clear /l/. We find a “dark l”


-before front vowels (e.g. left)


-a more velarized darker variety before back vowels (e.g. loose, low)


-the darkest one in postvocalic position (e.g. bolt, full)


-syllabic [l]


b. In WeE, the /l/ is always clear.


c. In AAVE, postvocalic /l/ may vocalize to [u] or [ʊ], as in bell [bɛl] or [bɛʊ], and /l/ may be deleted before a labial
consonant(e.g. help [hɛlp] or [hɛp], wolf [wʊlf] or [wʊf].


⑥Palatalization (Coalescent assimilation)


- 개념 : The alveolar obstruents of English /s, z, t, d/ become palatoalveolar /ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ/ when followed by a word that starts


with the palatal glide /j/.


-Alveolar sounds are produced at the alveolar ridge. Palatal glide is pronounced at the palatal region. Because of the influence
from palatal glide, alveolar sound is produced at the alveolar region which is more behind of alveolar ridge.


-this is often used in fast speech.


Ex. I miss you [aɪmɪʃju], I please you [aɪpliʒju], meet you [mitʃjə], did you [didʒjə], this year [ðɪʃjiɹ], confession [kənˈfeʃn],
pleasure [pleʒə(r)], stature[ˈstætʃə(r)], question[ˈkwestʃən], procedure[prəˈsiːdʒə(r)]


who’s your boss? [huʒjʊɹ bɑs], ate your dinner [eɪtʃjɚdɪnɚ]


⑦Palatalization and Labialization of velar stops


- Palatalization of velar stops: Velar stops /k, g/ are more front when before a front vowel than when before a back vowel


Ex. geese [gʲ], keep [kʲ], car key


- Labialization of velar stops: Velar stops /k, g/ are labialized when it is followed by a rounded vowel.


Ex. goose [gʷ], cool [kʷ]


⑧Dentalization of nasals


- 개념 : The alveolar nasal /n/ is articulated in a more forward fashion (dental) when it is followed by an interdental /θ, ð/


Ex. tenth /tɛnθ/ [tɛn̪ θ], when they [wɛn̪ ðe], ban the film [bæn̪ ðə fɪlm]


⑨Nasal devoicing


- 개념 : /m, n/ are partially devoiced after the voiceless obstruent /s/


-progressive assimilation 중 하나


Ex. snail [sn̥ el], small [sm̥ ɔl]


2) Dissimilation: change feature values to make two phonemes in a string more dissimilar.


①Fricative Dissimilation


- 개념 : The fricative /θ/ becomes dissimilar to the preceding fricative by becoming a stop


Ex. sixth [sɪkst], fifth [fɪft]


②Liquid Dissimilation


- 개념 : When the suffix ‘-al’ is added to a noun that contains the liquid /l/, the suffix is changed to ‘-ar’ and as ‘columnar’


illustrates, the /l/ need not be the consonant directly preceding the dissimilated segment


Ex. anecdotal, penal, spiritual, venal, annual, mental, coronal/ angular, similar, velar, columnar, perpendicular


3) Segment Deletion: delete phonemic segments in a certain context.

- 개념


a. Delete the unstressed vowels in casual or rapid speech.


Ex. mystery → mystry, general → genral, memory → memry


b. Schwa deletion occurs in fast speech when a preceding vowel is stressed and a following vowel is another schwa vowel.


Ex. camera [ˈkæməɹə] [ˈkæmɹə], principal [ˈpɹɪnsəpəl] [ˈpɹɪnspəl] (↔principality [pɹɪnsəˈpæləti])

3. Neutralization


- 개념 : the sounds that belong to two separate phonemes may lose their contrast in certain environment(s)


①Consonant Neutralization


a. /t/ vs. /d/ = Flapping, Tapping


-When we have /t, d/ in intervocalic position in unstressed syllables, the contrast between /t/ and /d/ disappears and they
are both realized identically as “flap” [ɾ]. That is, by the flapping rule, the phonemic contrast between /t/ and /d/ is lost
between a stressed and an unstressed vowel, resulting in the neutralization of the contrast in that position(2013 30).


Ex. writer – rider [ɹaɪɾɚ]


-AEP: Flapping(tapping) converts an alveolar stop /t, d/ to a voiced flap/tap in intervocalic position when the second
syllable is unstressed. It is valid across word boundaries.


Ex. attic, atom, metal, Italy, rhetoric, addict, dramatic, notable, autumn, at all, eat up, greater(2020A7), water, header, fatty,
daddy, competing(2016B2)


Although in a great majority of cases of flapping the first vowel is stressed, but this is not a necessary condition.


Ex. nationality [næʃənæləɾi], sorority [sərɔrəɾi], calamity [kəlæməɾɪ], monitor, humanity, comedy(2016B2)


Other environments:


(a) the r-coloring of the first vowel, ex. porter [pɔrɾɚ], border [bɔrɾɚ]


(b) the following syllabic liquid, ex. little [lɪɾl̩], cattle [kæɾl̩], bitter[bɪɾɚ], butter[bʌɾɚ], riddle, saddle, battle(2016B2)


b. the voiceless (/p, t, k/) and voiced stops (/b, d, g/)
-The contrasts between the voiceless and voiced stops are neutralized following /s/ in word-initial position. Although we
generally see the transcriptions of spit and skin as [spɪt] and [skɪn] respectively, they are not in any way different from

[sb..] and [sg..]


c. /ɪ/ and /ɛ/


-The contrast between the two front vowels /ɪ/ and /ɛ/ is neutralized in favor of [ɪ] if the consonant after the vowel is nasal.


-In African American English /ɪ/ and /ɛ/ are pronounced the same before nasal consonants. (201330)


Ex. [pɪn] vs. pen [pɛn] → the contrast is neutralized in favor of [ɪ]

-In some accents of English, /p, t, k/ in syllable final position are realized as a glottal stop. Ex. pit, tip, kick


②Vowel Neutralization (=r-coloring)(201330)


- 개념 : When the following /ɹ/ is in the same syllable (as in ear, cure, work), the vowel takes on some retroflex quality, which


is commonly known as “r-coloring”. When this happens, several otherwise well-established vowel contrasts of English are
neutralized.


i. High front /i/ and /ɪ/ - ear, fear, beard, pier


ii. High back /u/ and /ʊ/ - tour, mature, endure, poor, cure


iii. Front mid and low /e/, /ɛ/, /æ/ - Mary, merry, marry
